Preface

Kenya's coffee is a global treasure, supporting millions and earning billions. Smallholder farmers face low prices, pests, diseases, and climate change but can succeed with the right techniques. This guide offers practical, low-cost steps to boost yields, improve quality, and increase profits for farmers in regions like Nyeri or Kericho.

Start today to secure a better future through sustainable coffee farming

Introduction to Coffee Farming in Kenya

Coffee drives Kenya's economy, introduced in 1893, now the fifthlargest foreign exchange earner. Smallholders produce 70% of Kenya's prized Arabica coffee on approximately 209,035 acres, known for 3 bright acidity and fruity flavours. Challenges include volatile prices (as low as KES 20/kg), pests, diseases and climate change. Opportunities lie in global demand, sustainable practices, and cooperatives.

This guide equips you with modern techniques for profitable farming.

Selecting the Right Land and Variety

Ideal land: Choose altitudes of 1,300-2,100m with 15-25Â°C temperatures and 1,200-2,000mm rainfall.

Soil matters: Use deep, acidic volcanic soil(pH 5.5-6.5); test at agricultural offices.

Adjust soil: Add compost if too alkaline; avoid waterlogged land.

Clear land: Remove trees, shrubs, weeds during dry season(January-February).

Variety selection:

  1. SL28 & SL34: Excellent flavour, drought-resistant, prone to CBD and leaf rust.
  2. Batian: Disease-resistant, high yield, great quality
  3. Ruiru 11: High-yielding, CBD/leaf rust-resistant, reliable for smallholders.
  4. K7: Moderate yield, suits lower altitudes.
  5. Robusta: High yield, lower market value, rarely grown.

Mix varieties: Interplant Batian and Ruiru 11 for disease resistance and yield.

Certified seedlings: Buy from Coffee Research Institute or trusted nurseries.

Prepare land: Dig 60cm deep/wide holes, add two wheelbarrows of compost, space 2-2.5m apart, aerate 2-3 months.

Planting and Early Care

Plant in rainy season: Start March=May or October-November for moisture.

Use certified seedlings: Source from Coffee Research Institute for quality.

Planting method: Place one seedling per hole, straighten roots, firm soil, water well.

Mulch: Apply grass or coffee husks to retain moisture, suppress weeds.

Spacing: Keep 2-2.5m between plants, 648-809 trees/acre for sunlight and air.

Shade trees: Plant Grevillea or Leucaena 6-8m apart to protect young plants.

Watering: Water weekly in dry spells, use drip irrigation if possible.

Weed control: Hand-weed young plants; use Glyphosate (200ml/20L water) carefully.

Mulch care: Apply 10-15cm organic mulch, avoid stem contactto prevent rot.

Prune shade trees: Trim annually to balance light and shade.

First harvest timeline: Expected first harvest in 2-3 years, with Batian and Ruiru 11 yielding as early as 2 years , SL28/SL34/K7 closer to 2.5-3 years, depending on care and conditions

Soil Fertility and Nutrition

Test soil yearly: Check pH (5.5-6.5) and nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium).

Add organic matter: Use compost or manure to enrich poor soils.

Fertilize regularly: Apply NPK 17:17:17 (100-150g/tree) or organic options every 6-8 weeks in rainy seasons.

Avoid over-fertilizing: Protect roots and groundwater from harm.

Intercrop: Plant legumes like beans to fix nitrogen.

Recycle waste: Use coffee husks, pruned branches as mulch to retain moisture.

Join cooperatives: Access subsidized inputs to cut costs.

Pest and Disease Management

Common pests:

  1. Coffee Berry Borer (CBB): Use alcohol-based traps, remove fallen cherries.
  2. Thrips: Apply neem oil (50ml/20L water) or predatory mites.
  3. Antestia Bugs: Hand-remove or use pyrethrum sprays.

Major diseases:

  1. Coffee Berry Disease (CBD): Plant Ruiru 11 or Batian, apply Copper Nordox (50g/20L water).
  2. Leaf Rust: Prune for ventilation, use Dithane M45 fungicide.
  3. Bacterial Blight: Burn affected parts immediately

Organic solutions: Use neem or tithonia extract (1kg leaves/5L water, fermented).

Monitor weekly: Catch pests/diseases early to limit damage.

Improve airflow: Prune and space plants to reduce humidity and disease.

Pruning and Canopy Management

Prune annually: Trim post-harvest (January-February) to boost yield and airflow.

Use sharp tools: Clean cuts prevent infections.

Capping: Cut main stem at 1.8-2m for lateral growth.

Desuckering: Remove excess suckers to focus energy on fruit.

Thinning: Clear weak branches for light and air penetration.

Space trees: Keep 2-2.5m apart for balanced canopy.

Prune shade trees: Avoid over-shading to maintain yields.

Recycle prunings: Chop into mulch to enrich soil, reduce costs.

Harvesting and Post Harvest Processing

Harvest ripe cherries: Pick red cherries in main (October-December) or fly (April-July) seasons.

Hand-pick selectively: Avoid green/overripe cherries for best quality.

Yield potential: 0.81-2.02 tons/acre cherries, 400-1,000kg green beans.

Wet processing:

  1. Sort: Remove defective cherries by hand or flotation.
  2. Pulp: Use pulping machine within 24 hours.
  3. Ferment: Soak 12-36 hours to remove mucilage.
  4. Wash: Rinse thoroughly to clean beans.
  5. Dry: Spread on raised beds for 7-14 days (10-12% moisture).

Dry processing: Dry whole cherries 2-4 weeks for unique flavours.

Hulling: Remove parchment layer to get green beans.

Store properly: Use sisal/jute bags in cool, dry conditions, avoid plastic.

Marketing and Profitability

Market options:

  1. Nairobi Coffee Exchange: KES 120-180/kg for green beans via auctions.
  2. Direct trade: Earn KES 550-850/kg by selling to roasters.

Focus on quality: Selective picking/ processing ensures premium prices.

Get certified: Eco-friendly labels (e.g.Rainforest Alliance)attract buyers.

Join cooperatives: Access training, inputs, markets to cut costs.

Address challenges:

  1. Price volatility: Use direct trade for better prices.
  2. High costs: Adopt regenerative practices to save money.
  3. Climate impact: Use irrigation, shade trees to boost resilience.

Train youth: Invest in future to sustain your farm.

Climate Change and Sustainability

Climate threats: Rising temperatures, erratic rains risk 50% of coffee land by 2050.

Adapt with varieties: Plant Batian, Ruiru 11 for disease/drought resistance.

Use irrigation: Install drip systems for dry seasons.

Plant shade trees: Grevillea, Macadamia or Leucaena stabilize soil, reduce evaporation.

Harvest water: Build reservoirs to store rainwater.

Regenerative practices:

  1. Mulch and compost: Enhance soil with organic matter.
  2. Intercrop: Use legumes to fix nitrogen.
  3. Biodiversity: Grow cover crops to support pollinators, pest predators.

Get training: Join Coffee Research Institute or cooperatives for climate-smart skills.
